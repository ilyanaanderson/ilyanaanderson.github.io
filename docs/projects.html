Projects

  1. Prediction of Nucleotide Reactivity at Each Position in RNA Molecules Using Custom Transformer Architecture
    Understanding Ribonucleic acid (RNA) molecule structure is crucial in many fields of medicine. Experimental measurements of several types of chemical reactivity can be utilized to derive such structure information. In the present work, I used customized transformer architecture to predict two types of reactivity at each position of RNA molecules as part of the Stanford Ribonanza RNA Folding competition on Kaggle. I experimented with rotary and sinusoidal embeddings, as well as feeding different types of sequence-related data (features) into the decoder part of my transformer models. My best approach yielded 0.15867 mean absolute error (MAE) score on the private leaderboard test data. This solution placed in the top 8% of contest submissions.

  2. Exploration of Methods to Remove Bias From LLMs Fine-Tuned on Natural Language Inference Task
    In this project, I made several attempts to remove bias from the models trained for the natural language inference task, including two novel approaches. I explored the possibility that a model forced to generate a hypothesis based on a premise might be less prone to learning the dataset bias. Another attempt to reduce the learned bias was made by flipping premises and hypotheses for the contradiction examples during the training. Lastly, I implemented the DRiFT method reported in the literature. Most of the enhanced models demonstrated improved performance on the HANS dataset relative to the baseline results, suggesting a potential reduction in bias.

  3. Development of a neural-network-based agent to play SuperTuxKart ice hockey
    The project's objective was to create an agent capable of playing SuperTuxKart ice hockey by choosing an appropriate action at every turn. Working within a team, we pursued various strategies, including the development of a computer vision system from scratch and reinforcement learning. The focus of my work was to implement imitation learning and dagger algorithms. The team selected my agent design as the basis for further experimentation. This agent placed second in a class-wide competition.
